 Post "How Can I Help?" fliers in your neighborhood.
  • Flyering is easy. Just select the fliers below that are relevant to your community, download them, and print out as many copies as you can. Put the fliers in a backpack with a stapler (don’t forget extra staples) and some tape, and take the backpack with you whenever you leave the house for a walk, a bike ride, or a trip to the grocery store.
    • Fliers:
    • Racism happens here
    • What does "abolish the police" mean?
    • New Jersey is no exception
    • Redlining (Union County Map)
    • Redlining timeline
    • Redlining (Ta-Nehisi Coates Quote)
    • Racism happens here (Westfield NJ)
    • Racism happens here (Cranford NJ)
    • Where should I post fliers?
    • In residential areas: telephone poles, best visibility at street intersections
    • In local parks: on trees and posts along walking paths
    • In downtown areas: near parking meters, on trash cans
    • Local businesses: At the entrance and exit of grocery stores. *Ask permission before posting fliers on private property. Many local business owners are happy to give permission.
    • Transit: At the entance and exit of public transit (train stations, bus stops)
  • Fliers tend to get torn down within a few days. That’s why it’s good to incorporate flyering into your routine walks, bike rides, and grocery trips. If you post 100 fliers in 1 day and never go flyering again, you may reach fewer people than if you post 10 fliers on 10 days over the course of a month.
  • If you see a home with some outward sign of support— for example a rainbow flag, a peace flag, one of those “In this house we believe” or “All neighbors are welcome here” signs— consider leaving a flier in their mailbox with an introductory note and your contact info on it. Offer to share anti-racist resources with them. It will help you build a local anti-racist education network.
  • Avoid provocation and look out for your own safety while flyering— threats of violence have been made against flyerers online. COVID-19 is ongoing, so wear gloves when stapling or taping to surfaces in public.
  • Be mindful of Black community members when flyering— do not post anywhere that may endanger Black people or create additional stress for Black people going about their daily lives in majority-white communities.
  • Calling the police can amount to calling in an execution, so don't rely on the police for the safety of your family and your community.
    • You can put together a contact list of other people you can call. This might include:
    • Neighbors' phone numbers
    • Mental health crisis hotline for your area
    • Domestic violence crisis hotline for your area
    • Substance abuse crisis hotline for your area
    • Put the contact list on your refrigerator and reference it in emergencies. Next, read the guides below to learn how else you can reduce your reliance on calling the police.
